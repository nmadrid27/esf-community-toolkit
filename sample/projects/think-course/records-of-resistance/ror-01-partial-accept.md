# Record of Resistance: 01

**Project:** Visual Essay on Identity and Place | **THINK level | Jamie Torres, BFA Illustration**
**Date:** Week 4, Thursday

---

## What the AI Suggested

I was working on the composition for my third panel, the one about my grandmother's kitchen. I described the space and the feeling I wanted, and the AI suggested a layout: a central vanishing point drawing the viewer through the doorway, with warm objects (the comal, the radio, the hanging chiles) arranged symmetrically on either side to frame the entry. It also recommended a palette of burnt sienna, ochre, and deep terra cotta to "evoke warmth and cultural memory."

The composition idea was solid. The palette was wrong.

---

## What I Chose Instead

I kept the vanishing-point composition. It does what I need: it pulls the viewer into the space rather than letting them observe it from outside. That matches my position statement about making the viewer feel like they are standing in these places, not looking at photographs of them.

I rejected the color palette entirely. My grandmother's kitchen is not warm-toned in the way the AI assumed. The walls are mint green. The light is cool fluorescent mixed with afternoon sun through a small window. The warmth is in the objects and the memory, not in the color temperature. If I paint everything ochre and terra cotta, the piece becomes a stereotype of "Latin American domestic space" instead of a portrait of a specific room.

---

## Why

The AI defaulted to a cultural shorthand: warm palette equals warmth of memory. That is exactly the kind of visual cliche my position statement says I want to avoid. My non-negotiable is specificity. These are real places, not composites. The color has to come from observation and memory, not from a generalized idea of what a "warm" domestic scene looks like.

The composition, though, was genuinely useful. I had been struggling with how to make the viewer feel inside the room rather than looking at it, and the vanishing point through the doorway solved that problem in a way I had not considered. I can defend that choice on its own merits.

---

## What Happened

The mint green walls against the warm objects create a tension that works better than an all-warm palette would have. The fluorescent light gives the scene a specific, slightly unsettling quality that feels more honest. My professor pointed out that the color contrast between the cool walls and the warm comal actually draws the eye to the objects that matter most.

---

## Would I Change This Decision?

No. Accepting the composition while rejecting the palette felt like the right call, and the result confirms it. The AI was useful for solving a spatial problem. It was not useful for understanding what my grandmother's kitchen actually looks like. That knowledge is mine.
