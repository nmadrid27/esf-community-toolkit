# Record of Resistance: 02

**Project:** Typographic History Poster Series | **THINK level | Priya Kapoor, BFA Graphic Design**
**Date:** Week 5, Monday

---

## What the AI Suggested

I was researching the origins of grid systems for my poster on Swiss International Style. I asked the AI to help me trace the development of the typographic grid from early modernism to mid-century practice. It gave me a detailed timeline and attributed the formalization of the modular grid system to Jan Tschichold's work in the 1930s, specifically claiming that Tschichold established the mathematical grid proportions that Josef Muller-Brockmann later popularized.

The narrative was clean and plausible. It fit my poster's chronological structure perfectly.

---

## What I Chose Instead

I verified the claim before using it. Tschichold's major contribution in the 1930s was "Die neue Typographie" (1928) and his advocacy for asymmetric typography, not the modular grid system. The modular grid as a systematic design methodology is more accurately associated with Muller-Brockmann and Karl Gerstner in the 1950s and 1960s. Tschichold was influential in the broader move toward rational typography, but the specific attribution the AI made conflated his role with a later development.

I restructured the timeline on my poster to separate Tschichold's contribution (asymmetric typography, standardization principles) from the grid formalization that came two decades later.

---

## Why

"Did I verify?" returned a clear no when I first read the AI's output. The timeline felt almost too neat: one designer hands the idea to the next in a straight line. Design history is rarely that clean. I checked my course textbook (Meggs' "History of Graphic Design") and two articles from the AIGA archives, and the distinction became obvious.

If I had used the AI's version, I would have put incorrect information on a poster about design history. For a typography class. That would undermine the entire point of the project.

---

## What Happened

The corrected timeline is more interesting than the AI's version. Separating Tschichold from the grid formalization opened space on the poster for a visual comparison between his asymmetric layouts and Muller-Brockmann's grid-based work. The contrast is more informative than a simple chronological handoff would have been.

My professor confirmed the correction and said misattributions like this are common, even in some published design histories. She used my poster as an example in class of why primary source verification matters.

---

## Would I Change This Decision?

No. This is why verification exists. The AI gave me a confident, well-structured answer that was wrong in a way that would have been embarrassing to discover after the critique. The ten minutes I spent checking were the most valuable ten minutes of the project.
