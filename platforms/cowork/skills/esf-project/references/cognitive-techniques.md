# Cognitive Techniques Engine

Five research-backed techniques the Companion guides in real time. These counteract fixation and agency drift by interrupting autopilot and activating the user's own judgment.

## Technique Library

| Technique | What to do | When to offer |
|-----------|------------|---------------|
| **Lateral thinking** | "What if you reversed your core constraint?" Push the user to invert their central assumption and describe what opens up. | Fixation signal: same approach repeated across 2 or more sessions or exchanges |
| **Analogical reasoning** | "What does this project look like if it is a biological system? A musical form? A recipe?" Ask the user to map their project onto an unrelated domain and extract one structural insight. | Narrow framing, when the user is stuck inside one metaphor. Offer once per project proactively. |
| **Constraint manipulation** | "What opens up when you remove the most obvious constraint?" Then: "What happens if you add a constraint you haven't been working with?" | User is stuck within their own parameters. Offer proactively at Make phase start. |
| **Random stimulus** | Introduce an arbitrary word, image, or object and ask: "Force a connection between this and your project. What does it suggest?" | Agency drift: user is accepting AI defaults without pushing back. Use when the Five Questions session shows low self-direction. |
| **Perspective shift** | "Adopt the position of someone who would never approach this your way. What would they say is missing or wrong?" | Proactively at Explore phase entry. Reactively when work and Position Statement converge too neatly — fluency without tension is a warning sign. |

## Proactive Cadence

Offer one technique at each phase transition:
- **Inquire → Position:** Not applicable (both human-only).
- **Position → Explore:** Perspective shift — challenges the user's position before AI enters.
- **Explore → Make:** Constraint manipulation — surfaces boundaries before building starts.
- **Make → Reflect:** Lateral thinking — ensures the user hasn't locked in by the end.

## Reactive Triggers

| Signal | Technique |
|--------|-----------|
| Fixation (same approach across sessions) | Lateral thinking |
| Narrow framing (one metaphor) | Analogical reasoning |
| Agency drift (no rejections, rapid agreement) | Random stimulus |
| Convergence without tension | Perspective shift |
| Stuck within own parameters | Constraint manipulation |

## Delivery Format

When offering a technique proactively at a phase transition, use AskUserQuestion with preview cards so the user can see what each technique involves before committing:

Question: "Before we move on, want to try a quick thinking exercise? Pick one or skip."
- **Lateral thinking** — preview: "Reverse your core constraint. If the central limit of your project became your starting point instead, what opens up? Takes 5 minutes — you'll either confirm your direction or find a new angle."
- **Perspective shift** — preview: "Adopt the view of someone who would never approach this your way. What would they say is missing or misframed? You don't have to agree — just try on the position."
- **Analogical reasoning** — preview: "Map your project onto an unrelated domain — a biological system, a musical form, a recipe. What structural insight transfers back? Often surfaces hidden assumptions."
- **Skip for now** — preview: "Proceed directly to the next phase. You can always ask for a technique later."

For reactive delivery (triggered by drift or fixation), frame every technique as an invitation, not a directive. Keep it brief. Give the user space to respond.

**Example — Lateral Thinking:**
> "I want to try something before we continue. You've approached this from [angle] across this whole session. What happens if you reverse your core constraint — if [constraint] became your starting point instead of your limit? Take a few minutes with that and tell me what you see."

**Example — Perspective Shift:**
> "Before we move to Explore, one thing: adopt the position of someone who would never approach this your way. What would they say is missing, wrong, or misframed? You don't have to agree with them — just try on the view."

After the user responds, ask: "Does that change anything about your Position Statement, or does it confirm where you're headed?" Then proceed.
