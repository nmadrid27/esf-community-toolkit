# Process Blog: Week 5 Working Session

**Jamie Torres, BFA Illustration | THINK level, Project 2: Visual Essay**
**Date:** Week 5, Thursday

---

I spent today working on the library panel, the one about the Corpus Christi Bay public library, and I am realizing that my position statement is doing more work than I expected.

When I first wrote it, the position statement felt like a formality. I wrote it in about eight minutes, mostly because the assignment required it before I could use AI. But today, when I was going back and forth with the AI about how to handle the library's interior light, the position statement was the reason I noticed something was off.

The AI kept suggesting ways to make the library feel grand. Cathedral-quality light. Dramatic shadows. Wide perspective to show the scale of the reading room. And the reading room is not grand. It is a municipal building from the 1970s with drop ceiling tiles and fluorescent panels. The light is flat. The magic of that room was never visual. It was the fact that nobody bothered me there, and I could read for five hours without anyone asking what I was doing.

I do not know how to illustrate that. "Nobody bothered me" is not a visual quality. But my position statement says "specificity over sentiment," and the specific thing about that library is the drop ceiling, the industrial carpet, the smell of old binding glue, and the view of the bay through windows that probably have not been cleaned since I was ten. If I make it look beautiful, I am lying. If I make it look mundane, I am telling the truth, and the truth is more interesting.

So I rejected all of the AI's grandeur suggestions and started sketching the room as I actually remember it. Flat light, low ceiling, a table near the window. The water stain shaped like a boot is going on the ceiling tile in the upper-right panel. Nobody else in the world remembers that stain. It is mine.

This is what the Records of Resistance are supposed to capture, I think. Not dramatic moments where you heroically override the AI. Just the accumulation of small decisions where you notice the AI pulling you toward something generic and you pull back toward something specific. The position statement is what makes you notice the pull in the first place.

I did not expect the process to change how I think about the project. I expected it to be documentation I did after the fact. Instead, it is shaping the work in real time. I am not sure I would have caught the grandeur problem without the position statement sitting there reminding me what I actually care about.

Next session: start rendering the library panel. Flat light. Drop ceiling. Boot-shaped water stain. No drama.
