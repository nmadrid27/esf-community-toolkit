# Cohort Homogenization Detection

An opt-in feature for educators who want to identify when a group's thinking is converging toward similar directions — and surface that pattern before it becomes invisible.

---

## The problem this addresses

When a cohort of students all work with the same AI tools on the same brief, their outputs can converge in ways that are hard to see from inside a single project. Students do not copy each other. Each student may believe their thinking is independent. But if the same AI models are suggesting similar directions in response to similar prompts, the cohort may be producing work that is collectively narrower than the brief intended.

This is not a cheating problem. It is a cognition problem. The ESF framework calls it direction homogenization: the AI's probability distribution pulling a cohort toward similar choices without any individual student noticing.

Cohort analysis gives the educator visibility into this pattern before it reaches critique.

---

## How it works

**Entirely opt-in by students.** No student data is shared without explicit consent. The analysis reads only what students choose to share: their Position Statements, not their full project work.

**What gets analyzed:**

1. Students who consent share their Position Statement for a given project.
2. The educator (or the Companion acting on behalf of the educator) reads the collection and surfaces:
   - Shared vocabulary clusters: words, phrases, and framings that appear across multiple statements
   - Direction overlap: students converging on similar project directions despite different starting points
   - Boundary homogenization: students setting the same AI limits in similar language
   - Missing diversity: areas of the brief's concept space that no student is exploring

**What does NOT get analyzed:** Drafts, work in progress, session logs, Records of Resistance, or any content beyond the Position Statement.

---

## Running a cohort analysis

The educator collects Position Statements from consenting students and runs the analysis in a single session. The Companion does the work.

**Step 1: Collect statements**

Ask students to share their Position Statement file (`projects/[context]/position-statements/[project].md`) or paste its text. Consent can be a simple question at the start of a class session: "I am going to run a cohort analysis on your Position Statements to check for homogenization. If you would like your statement included, paste it in the shared document."

**Step 2: Run the analysis**

Paste the collected statements and ask the Companion:

> "Run a cohort homogenization analysis on these Position Statements. Surface shared vocabulary, direction overlap, and any areas of the brief that no one is exploring."

**Step 3: Review the report**

The Companion produces a cohort report structured as:

```
## Cohort Analysis: [brief name] — [date]

### Participation
[N] of [total] students contributed.

### Vocabulary Clusters
[Words and phrases appearing in 3+ statements with context]

### Direction Overlap
[Directions more than 2 students are pursuing]

### Boundary Patterns
[Common AI limits or constraints]

### Concept Space Gaps
[Areas of the brief that appear underexplored across the cohort]

### Signals for Discussion
[2-3 specific observations worth raising in class, phrased as questions not conclusions]
```

The report does not name individual students unless the educator wants attribution. The default is anonymous pattern analysis.

**Step 4: Use in class**

The most productive use of cohort analysis is a brief class discussion before students reach Phase 3 (Explore). Surface the overlap pattern without assigning blame:

> "I ran a quick analysis of your Position Statements. I noticed several of you are using similar framing around [X]. That might be fine — it might reflect something real about the brief. But I want to flag it before we start exploring. If your direction sounds like several others in the room, consider what you're not exploring."

This is not a call to change direction. It is a call to notice the pattern.

## What to do with homogenization signals

### If vocabulary clusters are high

High vocabulary overlap suggests students are drawing on the same sources (possibly the same AI outputs during Phase 1 research, or the same framing from the brief itself). Consider:
- Introducing constraint: "For the next session, your Position Statement cannot use any of these words: [list]."
- Encouraging analog research: books, physical references, studio visits.
- Broadening the brief's reference frame explicitly.

### If direction overlap is high

Multiple students pursuing the same conceptual direction is the core homogenization risk. Consider:
- Assigning perspective constraints: each student must locate one practitioner whose work contradicts their current direction.
- Running a "devil's advocate" session: students must argue against their own direction for five minutes.
- Requiring students to name one assumption behind their direction and interrogate it.

### If concept space gaps exist

If a significant area of the brief is unexplored by anyone, that may reflect AI's training distribution creating blind spots. Surface it explicitly:
- Show students the gap. Ask: "Why is no one exploring [area]? What is the assumption behind that gap?"
- This is often the most generative discussion a cohort can have.

---

## Privacy and consent

Cohort analysis involves student data. Follow these principles:

1. **Explicit opt-in only.** Never include a student's Position Statement without their explicit consent for this specific analysis.
2. **Anonymous by default.** Reports do not attribute statements to named students unless attribution is requested and students are informed.
3. **No storage.** The Companion does not store cohort analysis results. The educator saves or discards the report.
4. **Local only.** Position Statements shared for analysis should stay within the class context. Do not upload cohort data to external services.

---

## Relationship to ESF's epistemic goals

Cohort analysis is not about enforcing originality. It is about making visible a pattern that AI collaboration creates structurally. A cohort with high homogenization has not failed; it has encountered a predictable effect of using similar tools on similar problems.

The goal is collective epistemic diversity: the cohort produces a richer body of work when its members are genuinely exploring different directions. Position Statements that look like each other suggest the exploration phase may not have happened yet.

Surfacing this early — before Phase 4 (Make) — gives students the chance to diverge while there is still time to do so authentically.
