# ESF Community Workflow

## Why This Exists

Research shows that most people who use AI for creative or professional work gradually cede control over their own thinking. Not because they choose to, but because AI output is fast, fluent, and plausible, and evaluating it is slow and effortful.

The result is what researchers call "Cognitive Surrender": you delegate both the work and the judgment about whether the work reflects your intent (Kim et al., 2025). The creative act shifts from expressing your own ideas to selecting and refining AI-generated outputs (Aru, 2025). Your creative self-beliefs drop (Faiella et al., 2025). Your attention shifts from doing the creative thinking to monitoring AI's version of it, imposing metacognitive demands most people are not equipped to meet without structure (Hoßbach & Isaksen, 2025).

This is not a student problem. It is a human-AI interaction problem. It affects professionals, freelancers, researchers, and anyone who uses AI to produce work that matters.

ESF prevents this drift. It gives you three things no AI tool provides on its own:

- **A record of what you thought before AI weighed in** (Position Statement)
- **A structured way to build without losing control** (Build Practice: Define, Order, Check)
- **Evidence that you exercised judgment rather than accepting defaults** (Five Questions, Records of Resistance)

This is not a restriction on AI use. It is a structure for remaining the author of your own work.

## The Key Principle

The Position Statement is the gate. Without it, you have no anchor for deciding whether AI output serves your intent or replaces it. Everything downstream depends on knowing what you think before AI tells you what to think.

---

## Build Practice: Define, Order, Check

Build Practice structures Phase 3 (Build). It answers the question: "I have my Position Statement. Now how do I actually produce the work without losing control of the direction?"

### 1. Define

Before you start building, name each piece of your work. If you cannot name it clearly, you do not yet understand it well enough to direct it. Classify each piece by its epistemic weight:

| Weight | What It Means | Your Role |
|--------|--------------|-----------|
| **High** [H] | Original arguments, creative direction, strategic decisions, system design | Your thinking drives it. AI informs, not leads. |
| **Medium** [M] | Structure, technical implementation, supporting sections, documentation | Your judgment shapes it. AI can help draft and execute. |
| **Low** [L] | Formatting, boilerplate, administrative text, routine correspondence | AI handles it with light review from you. |

The act of defining is itself an exercise of creative authority. You decide what the pieces are, what weight they carry, and what "done" looks like for each one. AI does not make these decisions.

### 2. Order

Work the high-weight pieces first, when your Position Statement is freshest and your intent is sharpest. Identify dependencies: which pieces need other pieces to be done first, and which can progress independently.

Use whatever tracking method fits your practice: a task list, a project board, a document outline, a notebook. The method does not matter. What matters is that the ordering is visible to you and driven by epistemic weight, not by what AI can draft fastest.

### 3. Check

After completing each piece, ask: does this still reflect my Position Statement, or did I drift? This is a quick alignment check, not the full Five Questions. If you drifted, decide whether the drift was deliberate (update your Position Statement to reflect your evolved thinking) or accidental (correct course before starting the next piece).

Capture Records of Resistance during production, not after. When you reject or revise AI output on a piece, note it immediately. By the end of the project, these records are your clearest evidence that you directed the work rather than accepting it.

**Build Practice scales to the task.** For a quick email or short deliverable, the three moves may be a 30-second mental pass: name the parts, do the important one first, check it before moving on. For a major project spanning multiple sessions, they produce explicit artifacts: a piece list with weight classifications, a visible sequence, and per-piece check notes. The discipline is the same either way: define before you build, order by what matters most, check as you go.

## The Five Questions

Build Practice's Check move catches drift. The Five Questions catch something deeper: whether you are still the author of your own work. Apply them at major decision points, after completing a section, and before finalizing.

| # | Question | What It Tests |
|---|----------|---------------|
| 1 | **Can I defend this?** | Genuine understanding vs. surface familiarity |
| 2 | **Is this mine?** | Did I direct this, or accept AI's framing? (design authority test) |
| 3 | **Did I verify?** | Independent confirmation vs. assumed accuracy |
| 4 | **Would I teach this?** | Deep endorsement vs. shallow approval |
| 5 | **Is my disclosure honest?** | Transparent accountability vs. performative compliance |

**Check and the Five Questions work together.** Check is the quick pulse during building: "Am I still aligned with my intent?" The Five Questions are the full exam at the gates: "Can I defend this as mine?" You need both. Check without the Five Questions detects drift but not passive acceptance. The Five Questions without Check arrive too late to catch incremental erosion.

If any answer is **no**, revise, reduce AI contribution, or disclose accurately. The third option is not a failure. It is intellectual honesty.

---

## Records of Resistance

The most revealing evidence of your thinking is not what you accepted from AI. It is what you rejected.

For each significant moment where you reject, revise, or deliberately override AI output, document it:

- **What AI suggested**
- **What you chose instead**
- **Why** (referencing your Position Statement)

These take thirty seconds each. Over the course of a project, they become the clearest proof that you directed the process rather than following it.

---

## Working Across Sessions

For projects that span multiple AI sessions, briefly re-establish your Position Statement and key decisions at the start of each session. This is not repetitive. It is how you retain ownership of the project's memory.

What you choose to re-establish tells you what the highest-weight decisions are: the ones only you can hold across time. Keep a running note of what you re-introduce to AI at each session start. At the end of the project, that note is evidence of what stayed yours throughout.

---

## Content Epistemic Weight at a Glance

Not everything needs the full process. ESF scales to the stakes.

| Weight | Examples | What's Required |
|--------|----------|----------------|
| **High** | Original scholarship, strategic proposals, creative direction, system architecture | Full workflow: Position Statement, Build Practice with artifacts, Five Questions at every gate, disclosure |
| **Medium** | Reports, project plans, client deliverables, course materials | Standard workflow: Position Statement, Build Practice, key Five Questions checks, disclosure |
| **Low** | Emails, meeting agendas, formatting tasks, routine correspondence | Light workflow: quick Position mental note, skim for drift, attribution |
